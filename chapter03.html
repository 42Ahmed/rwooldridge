  1. What is the most likely sign for \(\beta_2\)? \[ bwght = \beta_0 + \beta_1 cigs + \beta_2 faminc + u \]

Interpretation:

\(\beta_2\) is expected to be positive. Higher family income \(\rightarrow\) better nutrition \(\rightarrow\) higher birth weight

  1. Do you think cigs and faminc are likely to be correlated?

Interpretation:

Yes, negative correlation. smoking rates is higher among lower-income demographics. Omitting family income will cause Omitted Variable Bias (OVB).

  1. Estimate the equation with and without faminc
model_bwght_simple <- lm(bwght ~ cigs, data = df_bwght)
model_bwght_multiple <- lm(bwght ~ cigs + faminc, data = df_bwght)

models_bwght <- list(
  "Without Income" = model_bwght_simple,
  "With Income" = model_bwght_multiple
)

modelsummary(
  models_bwght,
  fmt = 2,
  statistic = NULL,
  gof_map = c("nobs", "r.squared"),
  coef_rename = c(
    "cigs" = "Cigarettes/Day", 
    "faminc" = "Family Income ($1000s)", 
    "(Intercept)" = "Constant"
  )
)
Without Income With Income
Constant 119.77 116.97
Cigarettes/Day -0.51 -0.46
Family Income ($1000s) 0.09
Num.Obs. 1388 1388
R2 0.023 0.030

Interpretation:

Including income reduces the smoking coefficient from approximately \(-0.514\) to \(-0.463\). This indicates that the simple regression suffered from omitted variable bias. \[ \text{Bias} = \beta_2 \times \tilde \delta_1 \]

1. \(\beta_2\): Positive, rich people have heavier babies.

2. \(\tilde \delta_1\): Negative, poor people smoke more

3. Effect: Positive \(\times\) Negative = Negative Bias

4. \(\beta_1\) is negative, negative bias will increase it from -0.46 to -0.51

  1. What is the estimated increase in price for a house with one more bedroom, holding square footage constant?

Interpretation:

Holding square footage constant, adding one more bedroom increases price by $15,200. This estimate is not useful because adding small rooms without adding square footage will make it feel cramped.

  1. What is the estimated increase in price for a house with an additional bedroom that is 140 square feet in size?
base_house <- data.frame(sqrft  = 140, bdrms = 1)
upgraded_house <- data.frame(sqrft  = 280, bdrms = 2)

price_1 = predict(model_hprice, newdata = base_house)
price_2 = predict(model_hprice, newdata = upgraded_house)

price_increase <- price_2 - price_1

paste(round(price_increase, 2))
[1] "33.18"

Interpretation:

The estimates increase is $33,120. This represents the combined value of adding a room that is 140 square feet \[ 140 \times 0.13 + 15.2 = 33.2 \]

  1. What percentage of the variation in price is explained by square footage and number of bedrooms?

Interpretation:

63.2% of the variation in house prices are explained by the two variables.

  1. The first house in the sample has sqrft = 2,438 and bdrms = 4. Find the predicted selling price
new_data <- data.frame(sqrft = 2438, bdrms = 4)

result <- model_hprice$fitted.values[1]

Correlation = 0.78. This high correlation between market value and profits indicates multicollinearity. While this does not bias our estimates or inflate the overall \(R^2\), it makes it difficult to isolate the individual partial effects of profits versus market value.
