19.1 C1 [JTRAIN98]

Consider Example 19.1, where the outcome variable, earn98, is nonnegative. Use the data in JTRAIN98. Keep all of the data without trimming for lack of overlap. (i) What is the average value of earn98? For what percent of the sample is earn98 equal to zero? (ii) Use full regression adjustment with an exponential mean and Poisson QMLE to estimate \(\tau_{ate}\), using the same controls in Example 19.1. What is \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\), and how does it compare with linear RA in Table 19.1?

  1. Compare the standard errors of the linear RA and exponential RA estimates. Does the exponential RA improve precision in this application?
  2. Now use the exponential mean with IPWRA, and compare the estimate of \(\tau_{ate}\) with linear IPWRA in Table 19.1. Discuss the precision of the two estimates.
  3. If you focus on \(\tau_{att}\) instead, does the ranking of the estimators—linear mean versus exponential mean—change?

19.2 C2 [VIETNAM]

Use the data in VIETNAM to answer the following questions. These are a subset of the data used in Angrist (1990). (i) The binary variable vietnam indicates that a man served the United States military during the Vietnam war. How many men are in the entire sample? How many served? (ii) Estimate a simple regress with lwage as the outcome variable and vietnam as the only explanatory variable. Interpret the estimated coefficient on vietnam. Is it statistically different from zero? (Be sure to use a heteroskedasticity-robust statistic.) (iii) Using age, educ, and black as controls, apply linear regression adjustment (with interactions) to estimate the ATE and ATT of vietnam. Do you find any effect? (iv) Estimate a logit model for vietnam using age, educ, and black as explanatory variables. Is vietnam related to any of these variables in a statistically significant way? If so, describe how. How many fitted probabilities are less than 0.1? How many are greater than 0.9. Is lack of overlap a cause for concern in this data set? (v) Using the same controls in part (iii), obtain the IPWRA estimates using a logit model for vietnam and linear models for lwage. What are the new estimates of ATE and ATT? Do they differ much from those found in part (iii)? (vi) Add nonredundant interactions among all controls to both the logit and linear models, and obtain the IPWRA estimates. What are the new estimates of ATE and ATT? Overall, what do you conclude about the causal effect of serving in Vietnam on subsequent labor market earnings?

19.3 C3 [JTRAIN98]

Use the variable unem98 as the outcome variable in the data set JTRAIN98. The treatment variable is train. (i) Across all men in the data set, what is the unemployment rate in 1998? How does this rate differ among those who did and did not participate in the job training program? (ii) Using the same control variables in Example 19.1—including the squares and interactions of the variables—obtain the linear RA estimates of \(\tau_{ate}\) and \(\tau_{att}\) of the effect of train on unem98. Interpret the estimated effects and obtain the \(t\) statistics. (iii) Still using the same controls, use logit regression adjustment and report \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\) and \(\hat{\tau}_{att}\). How do these compare with the linear RA estimates? How do the standard errors of the logit RA estimates compare with the standard errors for linear RA? (iv) Use the same controls to estimate the propensity score by logit, and then obtain the IPWRA estimates of \(\tau_{ate}\) and \(\tau_{att}\) with a linear mean. How do these compare with the previous estimates? (v) Use two separate weighted regressions to obtain the fitted values in part (iv). That is, using only the controls, weight the linear regression by \(1/(1 - \hat{p}_i)\), and save the fitted values for all units. Then do the same for the treated units, where the weights are \(1/\hat{p}_i\). Difference the fitted values to obtain \(\widehat{te}_i\) for each \(i\), and then, to obtain \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\), average the \(\widehat{te}_i\) across all \(i\). What is the estimate? Is it easy to obtain a standard error? (vi) Now obtain the IPWRA estimates where the outcome models for unem98 are also logit. Do you find any notable differences and the ATE and ATT estimates compared with part (iv)?

19.4 C4 [VIETNAM]

Use the data in VIETNAM for this exercise. The binary variable draft is an IV for vietnam—which indicates serving for the U.S. military during the Vietnam war. (i) Regress draft on age, educ, and black. Does it appear draft is independent of these three variables? Explain.

  1. Estimate the first stage of 2SLS by regressing vietnam on draft, age, educ, and black. Discuss the coefficient on draft. Does draft seem suitable as an IV for vietnam?
  2. Estimate the equation \[lwage = \alpha + \tau \cdot vietnam + \beta_1 age + \beta_2 educ + \beta_3 black + u\] by instrumental variables, using draft as an IV for vietnam. Report the coefficient on vietnam along with its standard error.
  3. Estimate \(\tau_{late}\) using flexible equation (19.55). How large is the point estimate? What is the 95% confidence interval for \(\tau_{late}\)?
  4. Repeat part (iii) dropping all of the control variables and obtain the IV estimate of \(\tau_{late}\). How does it compare with the LATE estimate from part (iv)? Do you think this is a more convincing estimate than those found in part (iii) or part (iv)? Explain.

19.5 C5 [LABSUP]

Use the data in LABSUP to answer the following questions. Use \(z = samesex\), indicating whether the first two children are born the same sex, as an instrument for \(w = morekids\), indicating having more than two children. Drop the observations where \(multi2nd = 1\) (indicating that the second birth resulted in more than one child). (i) What is the percentage of women having two children? What is the percentage having more than two children? (ii) Regress \(morekids_i\) on \(samesex_i\). What is the estimated fraction of “compliers” in the population? (iii) Use samesex as an IV for morekids in an equation with hours as the outcome variable; do not use control variables. What is the IV estimate? How does it, and its precision, compare with the OLS estimate (difference in means) and its precision? (iv) Create centered versions of the control variables age, nonmomi, and educ, about their sample averages. Use linear regression adjustment of hours on samesex, the controls, and the interactions of samesex with the centered controls. What is the coefficient on samesex? (v) Repeat part (iv), but with morekids as the dependent variable. Is the samesex coefficient the expected sign? What is its heteroskedasticity-robust \(t\) statistic? (vi) Using hours as the outcome variable and treating morekids as endogenous, use the IV estimation in (19.55) to estimate \(\tau_{late}\). Report the estimate and its standard error. (vii) Confirm that the ratio of the samesex coefficients in parts (iv) and (v) equals the IV estimate from part (vi). (viii) Estimate a probit model for morekids, in place of the linear regression in part (v). Find the average partial effect of samesex. How close is it to the coefficient on samesex in part (v)? How do they compare in terms of statistical significance? (ix) Obtain the generalized residuals from part (viii) and implement the control function approach. How does the estimate compare with the IV estimate from part (vi)? Is there evidence that morekids is endogenous? What happens to the coefficient on morekids when the two generalized residual terms are dropped? (x) Can you think of reasons why samesex might not be exogenous (satisfy Assumption LATE.2’), even after controlling for age, nonmomi, and educ?

19.6 C6 [RDSENATE]

Use the data in RDSENATE to answer the following questions. This is a data set on senatorial elections in the United States, similar to the data set RDHOUSE used in Example 19.3. The goal is to estimate the effect of party incumbency on election outcomes over many elections in the U.S. senate. (i) What is the calendar span of the data? How is margin measured, and what is its average value? Provide the state number and year of the smallest value of margin in absolute value. (ii) Regress Dvote on margin. What is the estimated coefficient. How do you interpret it? Is it statistically significant? (iii) Define the binary variable Dwin to be one if \(margin > 0\). (Note that this refers to the Democrat winning in the prior election, and so it acts as the “treatment” variable.) Add the > variables Dwin and \(Dwin \cdot margin\) to the regression in part (ii). What is the coefficient on Dwin? How do you interpret this coefficient? Does its magnitude seem practically important? Statistically different from zero? (iv) Do you think the interaction term \(Dwin \cdot margin\) is needed in part (iii)? What happens to the coefficient on Dwin if you drop the interaction? (v) Redo the regression in part (iii) but only use observations with \(-30 < margin < 30\). How many observations are used? What happens to the coefficient on Dwin? Is the interaction term significant? (vi) Now choose the bandwidth using the Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012) method. What is the chosen bandwidth? What is the estimated treatment effect of party incumbency? (vii) Compare the estimates from parts (vi) and (v), and discuss the relative precision of each.

19.7 C7 [HEADSTART]

Use the data in HEADSTART to answer the following questions. The data are cross-sectional at the U.S. county level, and include variables from the 1960s and 1970s. Some counties were given assistance in implementing the anti-poverty program known as Head Start. The rule determining assistance turned out to be based on the poverty rate in 1960. A county received assistance if its poverty rate exceeded 59.1984. The key outcome variable is the mortality rate of young children, mort_age5_9, in years following the implementation of the national Head Start program. (i) How many counties are included in the data set? How many received assistance in setting up their Head Start program? How many counties have missing data on the variable mort_age5_9? For how many counties is mort_age5_9 equal to zero? (ii) Define the variable \(povrate\_cut = povrate - 59.1984\). Using all of the data (for which you have an observation for mort_age5_9), use OLS to estimate the equation \[mort\_age5\_9 = \beta_0 + \beta_1 assist + \beta_2 povrate\_cut + \beta_3 (assist \cdot povrate\_cut) + u.\] What is the estimated policy effect of assisting the rollout of Head Start? Is it statistically different from zero at the 5% level? (iii) In part (ii), what is the estimated coefficient on povrate_cut? Describe the intuition behind its sign. What is the estimated difference in mortality for a county with a 50 percent poverty rate and one with a 30 percent poverty rate? (iv) Redo part (ii) using only counties with \(povrate \ge 40\) and \(povrate \le 80\). How many counties are included on each side of the cutoff? How do the estimated effect of assist and its statistical significance compare with those of part (ii)? (v) Now use the Imbens and Kalyanaraman (2012) approach to choose the bandwidth. How many counties are used on either side of the cutoff? What is the estimated treatment effect (at the cutoff) and its \(t\) statistic? (vi) Starting with the same model and bandwidth as in part (iv), include controls for percent urban, percent black, and log of the population—all measured in 1960. Compare the estimated treatment effect and precision with the results found in part (iv). (vii) Use mort_age25_plus as the outcome (dependent) variable and estimate the same model, with the same bandwidth, as in part (iv). This is the mortality rate for people much too old to have participated in Head Start. Explain your findings, including why this acts as a “placebo” check on the analysis.

19.8 C8 [CATHOLIC]

Use the data in CATHOLIC for this question. As in Example 19.4, the goal is to try to estimate the causal effect of attending a Catholic high school on test performance. Here, when IV or CF methods are used, the binary variable, parcath, which indicates whether at least one parent is Catholic, is the single IV for cathhs, attending a Catholic high school. (i) How many students are included in this data set? How many attend a Catholic high school? In how many families is at least one parent Catholic?

  1. Run a simple regression of cathhs on parcath (including an intercept, of course). Does the estimated coefficient make sense? Discuss its interpretation and statistical significance.
  2. Run a simple regression of math12 on cathhs. Report and discuss the coefficient on cathhs and its statistical significance.
  3. Now use motheduc, fatheduc, and lfaminc as controls in using (full) linear regression adjustment. What is \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\), the estimated average treatment effect, in this case?
  4. Regress cathhs on parcath, the controls in part (iv), and the interactions between parcath and the controls. Do it two ways: without centering the controls and with centering the controls about their sample averages. Discuss the coefficients on parcath and explain the reason for the difference. Can you conclude parcath is a suitably strong IV for cathhs?
  5. Obtain the simple IV estimator with math12 as the outcome, cathhs as the treatment, and parcath as the IV. What is the estimate of LATE?
  6. Now estimate LATE adding the controls in part (iv). Use equation (19.55) to allow flexible estimation. What is \(\hat{\tau}_{late}\)? Report a 95% confidence interval for \(\tau_{late}\).
  7. Estimate a probit model for cathhs that includes parcath, the control variables in part (iv), and interactions between parcath and the centered controls. Find the average partial effect of parcath, and compare it with the appropriate estimate in part (v).
  8. Obtain the generalized residuals from the estimation in part (viii), and implement the CF regression in equation (19.90). How does \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\) compare with \(\hat{\tau}_{late}\) in part (vii)? (As a bonus, use bootstrapping to obtain a proper standard error for \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\).)
  9. In the CF regression, obtain \(\hat{\tau}_{att}\). How does it compare with \(\hat{\tau}_{ate}\)? (As a bonus, use bootstrapping to obtain a standard error for \(\hat{\tau}_{att}\).)

19.10 C10 [TUTORFRD]

(i) How many students are in the sample? How many students were initially eligible for the tutoring program? (ii) Of the eligible students, what percentage participated? Of the ineligible students, what percentage managed to participate? (iii) Estimate a sharp RD relating tutor to elig, restricting the regression to observations with \(40 \le score4 \le 80\). How many observations are used on each side of the cutoff? What is the estimated coefficient on elig? Interpret the interaction coefficient and discuss statistical significance.

  1. Sketch a graph similar to Figure 19.1, assuming that the tutoring program has a positive effect on score5. (Be careful; eligibility is determined here by being below the cutoff.)
  2. Use the IV approach to estimate \(\tau_{60}\), the ATE at the cutoff \(c = 60\). Use data satisfying \(40 \le score4_i \le 80\). What is \(\hat{\tau}_{60}\) and its standard error?
  3. Use the IK (2012) method to choose the bandwidth. What is the chosen bandwidth? How many total observations are used? How does the estimate of \(\tau_{60}\) and its precision compare with those from part (v)?
