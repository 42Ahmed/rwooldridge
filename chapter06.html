Interpretation:

In the multiple regression model, the coefficient for distance to the incinerator (\(\beta_1\)) shrinks considerably and becomes statistically insignificant. The large, significant estimate observed in the simple model was heavily driven by omitted variable bias; distance from the incinerator is positively correlated with other desirable, price-increasing characteristics, such as larger lot sizes and closer proximity to the interstate. By including these controls, the isolated effect of the incinerator is revealed to be much smaller. Furthermore, the fact that these newly added spatial variables also lack individual statistical significance strongly suggests multicollinearity. The model struggles to disentangle the individual marginal effects of these highly correlated geographic features, resulting in inflated standard errors.

Interpretation:

By adding the squared term for the distance to the interstate, we corrected a functional form misspecification present in the previous model. Both the linear term (2.07) and the squared term (-0.12) are statistically significant at the 1% level. The negative coefficient on the squared term indicates an inverted U-shaped relationship: moving away from the interstate initially increases house prices (escaping the noise penalty), but eventually, the marginal effect becomes negative as the house becomes too isolated (the commute penalty).With the non-linear spatial effect of the interstate properly modeled, the isolated effect of the incinerator is clarified. The coefficient \(\beta_1 = 0.19\) is statistically significant (\(p < 0.01\)). Holding all other controlled factors constant, a 1% increase in distance from the incinerator is associated with a 0.19% increase in the housing price.

Note

Optimal distance from interstate is obtained by solving

\[ \frac{\partial \log(price)}{\partial \log(intst)} = 2.07 - 2(0.12) \log(intst) = 0 \]

Which can be solved easily using the formula

\[ x^* = \left| \dfrac{\hat \beta_1}{2 \hat \beta_2}\right| = \dfrac{2.07}{0.24} = 8.625 \]

Where \(x^*\) is in log terms, so \(\exp{x^*} = \exp(8.625) = 5569\)

  1. Find the approximate return to the fifth year of experience, and 20 \[ \% \Delta \widehat{wage} \approx 100(\hat \beta_2 + 2 \hat \beta_3 exper) \Delta exper \]

The Math Way: \[\begin{align} 100(0.041 + 2(-0.001)\cdot 4) &= 3.53\%\\ 100(0.041 + 2(-0.001) \cdot 19 &= 1.3\%) \end{align}\]

The Code Way:

compare_df <- data.frame(
  exper = c(4, 5),
  educ = 12
)

log_wages <- predict(model_c2, newdata = compare_df)
log_diff <- log_wages[2] - log_wages[1]

pct_change_exact <- 100 * (exp(log_diff) - 1)
pct_change_approx <- 100 * log_diff

paste(pct_change_approx, pct_change_exact)
[1] "3.45868520562578 3.51919330198605"
Note

Why do my returns differ (3.53% vs. 3.45% vs. 3.52%)?

The discrepancy comes from how we handle the “curvature” of the Experience Squared term:

  1. The Math Way (3.53%): Uses the derivative (\(100 \cdot [\beta_2 + 2\beta_3 \cdot 4]\)). This is a linear approximation. It assumes the slope is a flat line at exactly Year 4. It ignores the “bend” that happens as you move toward Year 5.
  2. Approx Predict (3.46%): This is the Log Difference (\(\hat{y}_5 - \hat{y}_4\)). It accounts for the curve between the two years but stays in “log points.”
  3. Exact Predict (3.52%): This is the Exponential Change (\(100 \cdot [e^{(\hat{y}_5 - \hat{y}_4)} - 1]\)). This is the only mathematically “perfect” representation of the percentage increase in actual dollars.

Interpretation:

The estimated return to experience is positive but diminishing. Obtaining a fifth year of experience (moving from 4 to 5 years) is associated with an expected wage increase of approximately 3.5%, holding other factors constant. However, by the twentieth year of experience (moving from 19 to 20 years), the marginal return drops to approximately 1.3%

  1. At what value of experience does additional experience lower predicted log(wage)

\[ \begin{align} \% \Delta \widehat{wage} &\approx 100(\hat \beta_2 + 2 \hat \beta_3 exper) \Delta exper \\ 0 &= 100(\hat \beta_2 + 2 \hat \beta_3 exper) \Delta exper\\ 0 &= \hat \beta_2 + 2 \hat \beta_3 exper\\ 2 \hat \beta_3 exper &= - \hat \beta_2\\ exper &= \dfrac{- \hat \beta_2}{2 \hat \beta_3}\\ exper &= \dfrac{-0.04101}{2(-0.00071)} \\ &\approx 28.88 \text{ years} \end{align} \]
