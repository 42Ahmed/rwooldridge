  1. Find the average participation rate and the average match rate in the sample of plans
df_401k |>
  summarise(
    Avg_Part_Rt = mean(prate),
    Avg_Match_Rt = mean(mrate)
  ) |>
  round(2)
  Avg_Part_Rt Avg_Match_Rt
1       87.36         0.73

Interpretation:
On average, 87.36% of eligible employees participate in their company’s 401(k) plan. Their company matches $0.73 for every dollar an employee contributes.

estimate the simple regression equation \[\widehat{prate} = \hat \beta_0 + \hat \beta_1 mrate\]

model_401k <- lm(prate ~ mrate, data = df_401k)

modelsummary(
  model_401k,
  fmt = 2,
  statistic = NULL,
  gof_map = c("nobs", "r.squared"),
  coef_rename = c("mrate" = "Match Rate ($)", "(Intercept)" = "Constant")
)
(1)
Constant 83.08
Match Rate ($) 5.86
Num.Obs. 1534
R2 0.075

Interpretation: \[ \beta_0 = 83.03 \quad \beta_1 = 5.86 \quad R^2 = 0.075 \quad n = 1534 \quad \]

  1. Interpret the intercept in your equation. Interpret the coefficient on mrate

Interpretation:
If the match rate is 0, predicted participation rate is 83.08%.
For ever $1 increase in match rate, participation is predicted to increase by 5.86 percentage points.

  1. Find the predicted prate when mrate = 3.5. Is this a reasonable prediction?
new_data <- data.frame(mrate = 3.5)
pred_prate <- predict(model_401k, newdata = new_data)
round(pred_prate, 2)
     1 
103.59

Interpretation:
Model predicts a participation rate of 103.59% which exceeds maximum possible value of 100%

  1. How much of the variation in prate is explained by mrate? Is this a lot in your opinion?

Interpretation:
Match rate explains only about 7.5% of the variation in participation, while the remaining 92.5% are due to other factors.
